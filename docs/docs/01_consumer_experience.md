This section defines the production consumer experience for the iOS-first Covet app. Covet should be designed and built as a native-feeling mobile product optimized for people checking money on the go. Web may become a secondary surface later, but the production priority is iOS because the core use case is immediate financial reassurance: the user is out, considering a purchase, managing plans, or reacting to a notification, and needs to know whether they are good. Every screen should feel calm, minimal, intelligent, and finished. This is not a prototype flow. This is the primary consumer experience for the production app.
Onboarding
Onboarding must get the user to a trustworthy Safe to Spend number as quickly as possible without making the setup feel clinical or tedious. The flow should feel more like being fitted for a personal money manager than signing up for budgeting software. The user should never feel like they are building a spreadsheet. The required onboarding sequence is account creation, bank connection, calendar connection prompt, archetype quiz, initial context capture, Safe to Spend generation, and confirmation of detected recurring items or upcoming commitments.
The onboarding visual language should match Covet’s brand: chic, minimal, editorial, modern fintech, soft auras, restrained animation, generous whitespace, and precise typography. Use progress indicators when helpful, especially during the archetype quiz, but avoid making onboarding feel like a long form. The user should always understand where they are and why the information matters. Copy should be natural, slightly elevated, and never childish. Covet should ask for context like a thoughtful person, not a bank compliance form.
The onboarding flow should not ask users to manually create budgets. It should not ask for category limits. It should not ask the user to estimate every recurring expense if those can be detected from bank data. The purpose of onboarding is to connect the data sources, understand the user’s financial temperament, capture any immediate life context, and begin managing money decisions.
Account Creation
Covet must support account creation with phone number, email, and Apple Sign-In. The authentication implementation must be production-grade and must comply with current App Store approval requirements at the time of release. If third-party or external sign-in methods are offered, the implementation must be reviewed against Apple’s current sign-in, privacy, and data disclosure requirements before submission. Account creation should be secure, fast, and visually minimal.
The account creation screen should not feel like a growth funnel. It should feel like the start of a trusted financial relationship. Required fields should be kept to the minimum needed to create and secure the account. The app should support secure session persistence, password reset or passwordless recovery depending on the chosen auth architecture, and biometric unlock after account creation. Sensitive financial surfaces should be protected by device security where appropriate.
The user’s first name should be captured early because Covet uses personal language throughout the experience. The name should be used in the interface sparingly and tastefully. The product should not over-personalize every line of copy.
Bank Connection
Plaid is the default production bank connection layer. Bank connection is required for Covet’s full money management experience because Safe to Spend depends on real financial data. Manual mode is no longer part of the v1 consumer experience. If a user does not connect a bank, they should not be allowed to proceed into a fake full version of Covet that implies trustworthy money management. The app may provide a short explanation of why bank connection is necessary, but it should not offer a manual substitute that weakens the product promise.
The bank connection step should explain the value in plain language: Covet needs bank data to understand income, bills, spending habits, recurring charges, debt pressure, and what money is already spoken for. The copy should be direct and reassuring. Avoid generic language like “connect your accounts to view insights.” Better: “Covet needs your real transactions to tell you what you can safely spend.”
After Plaid connection, Covet should ingest available checking, savings, credit card, and transaction data as permitted by the user and supported by the integration. The initial processing step should identify current balances, recent income deposits, likely payday cadence, recurring bills, subscriptions, debt pressure, recent spending patterns, and obvious unusual transactions. This processing state should feel calm and intentional. Do not show raw loading logs. A short sequence such as “Reading your income,” “Finding what repeats,” and “Protecting what’s coming up” is acceptable if visually restrained.
The app must handle bank connection failure gracefully. If Plaid fails, the user should receive a clear explanation and the ability to retry, choose another institution, or return later. The product should not silently continue with unreliable data. If bank data is stale, incomplete, or disconnected, Covet must degrade confidence and communicate that clearly inside the app.
Calendar Connection
Calendar connection is strongly encouraged but not required. Covet should support Google Calendar and Apple Calendar. The calendar step should explain that Covet uses calendar context to understand upcoming life events that may affect spending, such as birthdays, trips, concerts, dinners, school events, appointments, travel, exams, and other commitments. The copy should make clear that Covet is not trying to analyze irrelevant meetings. It is trying to protect money for real-life events that may cost something.
Covet should parse event titles, dates, times, locations, notes, and travel context when available and permitted. It should identify whether an event is virtual or in person. Virtual meetings, Zoom calls, routine work calls, and low-spend calendar events should not automatically become financial commitments because that would create noise and annoyance. In-person events, travel-related events, ticketed events, birthdays, social plans, and academic milestones should be treated as candidates for Upcoming.
If an event location suggests meaningful travel distance, Covet should ask the user for mode of transportation rather than assuming cost. The interaction should be light. For example: “This looks like it may require travel. How are you getting there?” Options can include walking, public transit, rideshare, driving, train, flight, or not sure. This information should help Covet estimate whether transportation needs to be protected.
Calendar-derived commitments should not be silently protected without user confirmation. They should be surfaced with approve and deny controls, such as a check and X. If the user confirms, Covet can protect money for the event or ask for a missing amount if needed. If the user denies, Covet should remember that similar events may not matter financially unless future evidence suggests otherwise.
Archetype Quiz
Covet onboarding should include a six-question archetype quiz with a visible progress bar. The quiz should give users a sense of identity while helping Covet understand how strict, flexible, protective, or interpretive it should be when managing their money. The quiz should feel natural, posh, and human. It should not feel like a therapy intake or a budget personality quiz from a bank.
The archetype result should include one primary archetype and one secondary archetype. The user-facing archetypes are The Drifter, The Spontaneous, The Keeper, The Giver, and The Builder. Internally, these map to Avoidant, Spontaneous, Squirrel, Overgenerous/Santa Claus, and Investor. The public names should feel flattering but honest. The result should help the user feel seen, not diagnosed. The copy should never shame the user for avoidance, spontaneity, generosity, caution, or ambition.
The quiz should ask questions that reveal how the user behaves around money in real life. Questions should cover avoidance, impulse, safety-seeking, generosity, future orientation, social spending, and desire. Each question should be short and answerable in one tap. Example tone: “When money feels tight, what do you usually do?” or “What kind of purchase tends to get you?” The exact quiz logic belongs in the Financial Intelligence section, but the consumer experience should make the quiz feel elegant and useful.
After the quiz, Covet should show a concise archetype result. For example: “You’re The Spontaneous, with a little Keeper. You follow desire quickly, but you still want to feel safe. Covet will help you enjoy the moment without accidentally borrowing from next week.” The result screen should not be too long. It should transition quickly into Safe to Spend setup.
Initial Context Capture
In addition to the quiz, Covet should capture immediate context that bank and calendar data may not know yet. This should be limited and lightweight. The app may ask what the user is trying to do with money right now, what important expense or event is coming up, and how strict Covet should be. These questions should be phrased naturally and should not feel like budget setup.
The goal question should support options such as stop overspending, build a cushion, pay down debt, know what I can spend, save for something, or stay on track. The strictness question should affect buffers and tone, but should not make users feel punished. The upcoming context question should allow free text because users may mention things like “birthday next week,” “exam week,” “need a new ID,” “Pilates membership,” “trip to Austin,” or “brunch every weekend.” Covet should parse this context and turn relevant items into candidate commitments or habits.
Home
The Home screen is the emotional and functional center of Covet. Its job is to answer “Am I good?” in under three seconds. The screen should show the Covet wordmark, the current status, Safe to Spend number, daily pace if relevant, the wallet visualization, and a simple line about protected commitments. It should not show charts, category breakdowns, long explanations, net worth widgets, or financial clutter.
Safe to Spend must always show a dollar amount. A phrase alone is not useful. The user needs the number. The number should be visually dominant and easy to understand at a glance. The phrase around it can be minimal, such as “Safe to Spend” and “this pay cycle.” The home status should use clear states including YOU’RE GOOD, TAKE IT EASY, WAIT UNTIL PAYDAY, and LET’S NOT…. These statuses should map to the Safe to Spend engine and notification severity, but the UI should remain calm.
The wallet visualization should reflect the user’s financial state without becoming gimmicky. It can use color, glow, material, or subtle motion to communicate status. It should feel like the avatar of the user’s financial condition. The wallet should not replace the number; it should support the number emotionally. If the user taps the wallet or protected commitments line, the app can reveal the details behind what is protected, but the default home state should remain simple.
The Home screen should never show the sausage by default. It should present the conclusion first. If the user wants to understand why Safe to Spend changed, they can tap into details, Activity, or an explanation surface. The default experience should feel like checking financial weather: status, number, reassurance, done.
Activity
Activity is not a notification log and should not include useless entries like “notification sent.” Activity should show what matters after it has happened or after Covet has acted on it. The production Activity structure should follow the UI direction of Insights, Actions, and Transactions. These sections should help the user understand what Covet has noticed, what needs attention, and what actually happened financially.
Insights are the behavioral finance layer. They should not populate until the user has at least 25 transactions inside Covet. Before that threshold, the Insights section should either be hidden, softly empty, or say that Covet is still learning. Insights must be unique to the user and grounded in actual data. They should never feel generic. They should not cite books or frameworks to the user, even if the internal Insights Agent is informed by behavioral finance sources. The user should receive a clean Covet observation, not a lecture.
Actions are items that need user attention. This can include confirming a detected recurring habit, approving or denying a calendar-derived event, reviewing an unusual bill, reconnecting an account, or confirming a payday pattern. Actions should be short, obvious, and dismissible. The app should avoid creating busywork. If an action does not materially improve Covet’s ability to manage the user’s money, it should not appear.
Transactions should show a clean chronological list of financial activity. This section should not feel like accounting software. Each transaction should be readable and minimal, with merchant, amount, date, and any relevant Covet interpretation if needed. Transactions are not the hero of the product. They exist to support trust and review.
Upcoming
Upcoming is where Covet shows what it is planning around. It should be organized into Upcoming Events, Recurring, and Vaults. Upcoming Events includes calendar events, travel, plane tickets, manually inputted events, birthdays, exams, social plans, and other time-based commitments. Recurring includes bills, subscriptions, and recurring habits such as Pilates, brunch, weekly coffee, or other repeated lifestyle spending. Vaults include user desires or planned purchases such as a camera, jacket, trip, laptop, or other saved goal.
Upcoming Events should make it easy for users to approve or deny detected commitments. A detected birthday dinner, concert, trip, exam week, or travel plan should appear as a candidate with check and X controls. If confirmed, it becomes protected or partially protected depending on available funds, due date, and confidence. If denied, it should be removed or deprioritized.
Recurring should combine bills and habits in one section, but each item should be labeled clearly as bill, subscription, or habit. A Pilates class around $50, brunch every weekend around $75, rent, Spotify, insurance, and regular Sunday coffee can all live in Recurring because they repeat and affect Safe to Spend. Covet should learn these from transactions first and ask for confirmation when confidence is sufficient. The user should be able to approve, deny, edit amount, edit cadence, or pause.
Vaults should represent desired future purchases or plans. A vault is not a budget envelope in the traditional sense. It is a desire Covet is helping the user plan around. The Vaults section should show name, target amount, and expected date when possible. Vaults should eventually connect to purchase checks, sale alerts, and affordability timing, but they should not clutter the Home screen.
Pattern Review
Pattern Review should not be a separate heavy onboarding step. Covet should reveal patterns naturally through Recurring, Actions, and Upcoming as the system detects them. The user should confirm or deny patterns in context. This is better than forcing users through a review queue before they see the product.
Patterns can include recurring bills, subscriptions, payday cadence, weekend brunch, Pilates, higher exam-week spending, beauty appointments, social spending spikes, travel-related spending, or repeated merchant behavior. Pattern confirmation should use lightweight controls: check, X, and edit. The copy should be specific. “Looks like brunch usually lands around $75 on weekends. Plan around this?” is better than “Recurring dining pattern detected.”
A confirmed pattern should immediately improve the financial engine. A denied pattern should reduce confidence in similar future detections. An edited pattern should update amount, cadence, merchant, category, or context. The user should feel that Covet is learning from them without forcing them to maintain a budget.
Purchase Check
Purchase Check should be a bubble/chat feature, not a rigid form. The user should be able to ask naturally, “Can I buy this $180 jacket?” or “Can I do Pilates today?” or “Can I book this flight?” The ideal v1 should support typed text, screenshots, and links because real purchase behavior is often visual and impulsive. If screenshot and link parsing cannot be fully automated at launch, the interface should still be designed to support them, and the product should extract item, price, merchant, and context when possible.
The Purchase Check response should return a clear decision: yes, wait, or no. The language can map to Covet’s status tone: “You’re good,” “I’d wait until Friday,” or “Let’s not.” The explanation should be concise and grounded. It should mention the relevant reason without exposing the full calculation. For example: “You can buy it. Rent and your birthday dinner are still covered.” Or: “I’d wait until payday. This would leave your weekend too tight.”
Purchase Check should update Activity and, when relevant, Vaults or Recurring. If a user repeatedly checks similar purchases, Covet should learn that desire or habit. If a user asks about a purchase and does not buy it, Covet may ask whether to save it as a Vault. If the user buys it, Covet should update Safe to Spend and future recommendations.
Notifications
Notifications are part of the consumer experience, but notification mechanics will be defined in detail in the Notification Engine section. At the experience level, Covet should only notify when something important changes or when the user needs to act. The product should not send engagement notifications for the sake of engagement. Silence is a feature.
Notification preferences must live in Settings from day one. Users should be able to control quiet hours, notification strictness, and categories of notifications. However, these settings should not become complicated. The default should be smart and restrained. Covet should earn permission to interrupt.
The notification tone should match the app. It should be short, human, and useful. Examples include “You’re good. Safe to Spend is now $416 after payday,” “Take it easy today. Your weekend pace is running high,” “Your concert is covered,” or “I’d wait until Friday for that.” Notifications should never sound like bank alerts unless legally required.
Settings
Settings should include accounts, calendar, notification preferences, financial goals, strictness, privacy and data, security, and profile. Settings should be clear but not overbuilt. Users should be able to reconnect bank accounts, manage connected calendars, update goals, adjust strictness, configure notification preferences and quiet hours, review privacy/data controls, manage security and biometric unlock, and edit basic profile information.
Settings should also include a way to review or reset confirmed patterns, archetype result, and recurring items. This should not be prominent enough to encourage constant tinkering, but it must be accessible for trust and correction. If Covet is wrong, the user needs a clean way to fix it.
Privacy and data controls are especially important because Covet uses sensitive financial and calendar context. The app should explain what data Covet uses and why in plain language. The product should never make users feel tricked into sharing more than necessary. Trust is part of the interface.
Consumer Experience Acceptance Criteria
A production user can create an account through phone, email, or Apple Sign-In, connect their bank through Plaid, connect or skip calendar, complete a six-question archetype quiz, receive a primary and secondary archetype, and land on a Home screen with a real Safe to Spend number. The Home screen must answer “Am I good?” in under three seconds. Safe to Spend must always be shown as a dollar amount. The app must not show category budgeting, charts, or dashboard clutter on Home.
A production user can review Upcoming Events, Recurring items, and Vaults. Calendar-derived commitments must appear with approve or deny controls before being fully protected. Recurring must include both bills and lifestyle habits. Activity must follow the structure of Insights, Actions, and Transactions. Insights must not appear until there are at least 25 transactions and must be generated from user-specific behavior, not generic templates.
A production user can ask Purchase Check through a chat-like bubble interface using natural language, and ideally through screenshots or links. Covet must return a clear yes, wait, or no decision with a concise explanation. Settings must include accounts, calendar, notifications, goals, strictness, privacy/data, security, and profile. Notifications must be configurable and must only exist to surface meaningful changes or required actions.
The entire consumer experience should make users feel that Covet is managing their money decisions for them. The user should not feel like they are budgeting, tracking, or interpreting a dashboard. The product should feel calm, tasteful, protective, and intelligent.
