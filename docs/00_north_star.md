0. North Star


What Covet Is
Covet is a production-grade consumer money management app that manages spending decisions for the user. It connects to the user’s financial accounts, calendar, income patterns, spending habits, bills, and life context to answer one primary question: “Am I good?” Covet’s core output is not a budget, a dashboard, or a balance. Its core output is judgment: whether the user can safely spend, whether they should wait, and whether something important has changed. The app should feel like a quiet money manager working in the background, not a tool that asks the user to become better at budgeting.
Covet should continuously interpret the user’s financial life and convert that complexity into simple decisions. It should understand how much money the user has, what money is already spoken for, what is coming up, how they typically spend, when they get paid, what commitments need to be protected, and how strict or flexible the system should be. The product should make the user feel that someone competent is watching over their money and only surfacing what matters.
Covet is notification-first. The user should not need to open the app every day to manage their money. Most of the time, Covet should stay silent. When something materially changes, Covet should notify the user with a short, useful message. The app itself exists as a place for reassurance, review, and decision-making when the user wants to check in or ask whether they can buy something.
The home screen exists to answer “Am I good?” in under three seconds. It should show the user’s current status, Safe to Spend amount, daily pace if needed, wallet state, and a simple reassurance line about protected commitments. It should not expose all underlying calculations. The intelligence should exist underneath the interface, not crowd it.
What Covet Is Not
Covet is not a budgeting app. It should not ask users to create category budgets, manually assign every dollar, or repeatedly maintain a financial plan. Covet should not make users feel like they are doing homework. Traditional budgeting products assume the user wants control through detail. Covet assumes the user wants relief through delegated judgment.
Covet is not an expense tracker. Transactions may appear in Activity, but tracking is not the product’s purpose. The purpose is to understand spending behavior and use that understanding to make better decisions for the user. Covet should not celebrate raw visibility for its own sake. Showing the user where money went is only useful if it helps Covet protect what matters or improve future decisions.
Covet is not a fintech dashboard. The app should not default to charts, graphs, spending donuts, net worth tiles, category breakdowns, or crowded analytics. Those patterns make users feel like they are responsible for interpreting their own finances. Covet’s job is to interpret first and present the conclusion.
Covet is not a chatbot-first financial coach. Conversational input may exist, especially for purchase checks and life context, but the product should not depend on users asking the right questions. Covet should proactively understand, decide, and notify. The user should feel supported even when they do nothing.
Covet is not a bank, investment advisor, tax advisor, lender, or credit counselor in v1. It should not make regulated claims or provide advice outside the scope of daily money management and spending decisions. It should help users understand what they can safely spend based on their own financial data, habits, obligations, and stated goals.
Core Promise
Covet keeps an eye on your money, protects what’s coming up, and tells you when you’re good to spend. This is the clearest external promise and should guide the product, brand, onboarding, notifications, and interface.
The internal product promise is stronger: Covet manages the user’s money decisions so they do not have to budget. The app should reduce the number of times a user checks their bank account, mentally calculates affordability, worries about upcoming bills, or feels uncertain before spending. Covet should make money feel less present in the user’s mind.
The product should earn trust by being calm, precise, and restrained. It should not over-notify, over-explain, over-gamify, or over-design. Every major interaction should leave the user with more clarity than they had before. The ideal Covet interaction is short: the user opens the app or receives a notification, understands their status, and moves on with their life.
Target User
The primary user is a Gen Z or young millennial consumer who has income, bills, social plans, subscriptions, spending habits, and money anxiety, but does not want to actively budget. They may be a student, early-career worker, freelancer, service worker, creator, intern, or young professional. They are not necessarily financially illiterate. They are mentally overloaded. Their problem is not always lack of information; it is the constant cognitive burden of deciding whether they are okay.
This user often thinks in practical questions: Can I buy this? Am I okay until payday? Did I spend too much this weekend? Is rent covered? Can I go to dinner? Should I wait? They may check their bank account often, but the balance alone does not answer the real question because some of that money is already committed to bills, plans, debt, or future obligations.
The user values simplicity, taste, and emotional reassurance. They do not want an app that feels childish, punitive, overly corporate, or cluttered. They want something that feels intelligent, stylish, and quietly protective. Covet should feel like a product made for people with taste who still need help managing everyday money.
The early target user is not someone who wants complex financial planning, investment management, tax optimization, or full household accounting. The early target user wants to stop thinking so hard about daily spending while still feeling in control.
Core Use Case Examples
Covet should understand recurring life patterns, not just transactions. A user should not have to manually budget for every repeating behavior. If Covet sees that the user takes Pilates most weeks for around $50, it should treat that as a lifestyle habit and quietly account for it inside Safe to Spend. If the user usually goes to brunch on weekends and spends around $75, Covet should begin protecting for that pattern before the weekend arrives. The goal is not to shame the user for having habits. The goal is to make the user’s real life financially legible so Covet can manage around it.
Covet should also understand calendar-based spending. If the user has a birthday dinner coming up, Covet should anticipate spending for the gift, dinner, transportation, or buffer depending on the user’s past behavior and any context they provide. If the user has a concert next Friday, Covet should protect the ticket, expected food, merch, transportation, and a small cushion if those are relevant. If the user has New York Tech Week, a trip, a school event, or a social weekend coming up, Covet should treat those as future commitments, not surprises.
Covet should understand academic and seasonal context. If the user enters exam week, finals week, midterms, or a stressful work period, Covet should expect that spending may shift. The user may order more food, Uber more often, buy coffee more frequently, pay for wellness classes, or make convenience purchases. Covet should not require the user to pre-budget each of these. It should detect the context, compare it to past behavior, and adjust Safe to Spend or send a rare notification if the change is material.
Covet should account for irregular but predictable life expenses. These include birthdays, IDs, application fees, school supplies, beauty appointments, travel deposits, medical copays, gifts, events, and administrative expenses that do not happen every week but still affect whether the user is “good.” These should appear in Upcoming when known, and Covet should protect for them progressively based on urgency, due date, amount, and confidence.
Covet should treat habits differently from emergencies. A recurring Pilates class, weekend brunch habit, Sunday Tatte order, or monthly subscription should be learned as part of the user’s normal lifestyle. A sudden large bill, unexpected ID replacement, higher-than-usual utility bill, or urgent travel cost should be treated as a material change. Habits should quietly shape Safe to Spend. Major changes should trigger notifications only when the user needs to know.
Covet should never make the user feel like they need to manually maintain these patterns. The product should detect, suggest, and ask for lightweight confirmation. For example, Covet may say, “Looks like brunch is usually around $75 on weekends. Plan around this?” or “Exam week usually means higher food spend. I’ll keep things a little tighter this week.” The user should be able to confirm, deny, or edit the pattern in one tap.
Covet’s intelligence should improve over time. Early in the user journey, Covet may rely on bank transactions, calendar events, and 2–3 context questions. Over time, it should learn which events actually change spending, which habits are consistent, which expenses are optional, and which commitments matter most. The more Covet learns, the less the user should have to explain. The product should feel like it gradually understands the user’s life without turning that life into a spreadsheet.
Success Metrics
Covet succeeds when users think about money less while making better spending decisions. The primary success metric is not daily active usage for its own sake. A user opening the app less often can be a positive signal if they trust Covet’s notifications and Safe to Spend judgment.
The most important product metric is Safe to Spend trust. Users should believe the number is accurate, useful, and worth following. This can be measured through repeated usage of the home screen, purchase checks, notification engagement, and user feedback after major financial changes. If users ignore Safe to Spend or repeatedly feel surprised by bills, the product has failed.
Another core metric is reduced bank-checking behavior. Covet should replace the habit of opening a banking app multiple times per day with a calmer habit of checking Covet only when needed. The user should begin to rely on Covet as the interface for financial reassurance.
Notification quality is also a primary success metric. Covet should maintain high notification trust by only sending alerts for meaningful changes. Useful notifications should lead to user action, reassurance, or confirmed understanding. Unnecessary notifications are product failures.
Commitment protection is a core operational metric. Bills, subscriptions, events, trips, and stated goals should be detected, confirmed, and protected accurately. The user should feel that Covet remembers what is coming up and plans around it automatically.
Purchase decision completion is another key metric. When users ask whether they can buy something, Covet should return a clear yes, wait, or no with a concise reason. The decision should feel fast, confident, and grounded in the user’s real financial situation.
Non-Goals
Covet v1 will not build traditional category budgeting. Users should not be asked to create dining, shopping, grocery, or entertainment budgets manually. Spending categories may exist internally for intelligence, but they should not become the main user experience.
Covet v1 will not build a dense financial dashboard. The home screen should not include charts, net worth graphs, multi-account tables, spending donuts, monthly reports, or analytics widgets. If deeper details exist, they should live behind progressive disclosure and never compete with the core status.
Covet v1 will not optimize for maximum app engagement. The goal is not to make users scroll, browse, or obsess over money. The product should be useful even when the user rarely opens it.
Covet v1 will not provide investment advice, tax advice, formal credit counseling, or regulated financial planning. The product should remain focused on daily money management, spending decisions, commitment protection, and financial reassurance.
Covet v1 will not move money automatically without explicit future product approval. The first production version should manage decisions, not autonomously transfer funds, pay bills, cancel subscriptions, or execute financial actions. The architecture should allow these capabilities later, but the current product should not depend on them.
Covet v1 will not become a generic AI assistant. The product should not answer every possible financial question just because it can. Covet should stay focused on the daily user problem: understanding whether the user is financially okay and what they can safely do next.
Covet v1 will not use fear, shame, or aggressive warning language. The product should never make users feel punished for spending. It should guide, protect, and redirect with calm confidence. “I’d wait until Friday” is Covet. “You exceeded your budget” is not.
Covet v1 will not show the sausage unless the user asks. The system should calculate deeply, but the interface should remain simple. The product should present conclusions first and explanations second. The default experience should feel like judgment, not accounting.
