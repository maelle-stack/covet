This section defines future product directions Covet’s architecture should not block. These systems are not part of v1 unless explicitly scoped later. Claude/Codex must not implement them now, create placeholder screens for them, or pollute the v1 user experience with unfinished future features.
Future systems include expanded Shopping and Vaults, concierge actions, subscription cancellation, auto-save, auto-pay, a Covet card, and possible future investing or financial intelligence products. The v1 architecture should preserve clean extension points for these systems through stable models such as User, Transaction, Commitment, RecurringItem, Vault, PurchaseCheck, SafeToSpendSnapshot, Notification, Pattern, and Insight.
Shopping and Vaults are the most natural first extension because they already connect to Purchase Check and the Covet philosophy of desire without regret. In v1, Vaults should only support saving desired items, tracking affordability, and optionally protecting money when the user actively chooses. Future versions may support price tracking, sale alerts, affiliate offers, merchant links, recommendations, and “you can buy this now” notifications. Shopping must never become random promotion. Covet should help users buy better, not buy more.
Concierge is a future action layer. It may eventually help users handle financial admin, shopping decisions, subscription cleanup, travel planning, or broader life logistics. In v1, Covet should advise and protect; it should not take autonomous real-world actions beyond notifications and recommendations. Any future concierge action should require explicit user permission until the product has earned deeper trust.
Subscription cancellation is a future system. Covet may eventually detect unused or harmful recurring charges, recommend cancellation, and help cancel them. In v1, Covet should detect recurring subscriptions and include them in Recurring, Safe to Spend, and pattern confirmation. It should not build cancellation workflows yet.
Auto-save is a future money movement system. Covet may eventually move money into protected pools, vaults, or savings destinations automatically. In v1, Covet should recommend and reserve conceptually; it should not move money. Architecture should allow future money movement but should not depend on it.
Auto-pay is also future. Covet may eventually pay bills or debt minimums directly, but this introduces higher trust, compliance, and failure-risk requirements. In v1, Covet should protect money for bills and remind the user when needed. It should not pay bills directly.
A Covet card is a future enforcement layer. It could eventually make Safe to Spend real at the point of purchase by approving, warning, declining, or routing spend according to Covet’s engine. In v1, credit and debit card issuing should remain out of scope. The architecture should keep Safe to Spend and Purchase Check modular enough that a card layer could use them later.
Investing and financial intelligence products are future company-level opportunities, not v1 product features. The consumer app should not include investing advice, trading, portfolio recommendations, or institutional data products in v1. Any future data or research product must be privacy-preserving, permissioned, aggregated, and designed so users never feel their personal financial life is being exploited.
The rule for Part 6 is simple: do not build these systems now. Preserve the architecture so they can be added later without rewriting the core. If a future feature does not reduce the user’s cognitive load around money, it should not enter the product.
